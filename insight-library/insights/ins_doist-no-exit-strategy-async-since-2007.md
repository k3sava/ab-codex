# Doist rejected an exit strategy in 2009 and has been async-and-remote-first since founding

## Claim
Amir Salihefendić founded Doist in 2007, declined a 2009 VC offer that would have replaced him as CEO, and has run the company bootstrapped, profitable, and async-and-remote-first ever since. Doist publishes 97% employee retention with 50% of staff at four-plus years' tenure.

## Mechanism
Refusing an exit strategy removes the forcing function that pushes founders into mercenary growth, dilution, and acquisitions that "outright kill" the company. Async-first communication compounds talent because operators with high writing fluency and self-direction stay where the work-life-control tradeoff favors them. Long tenure compounds product depth because contributors retain context across years, not quarters.

## Conditions
Holds when the founder is willing to forgo the optionality and personal liquidity that come with an exit path.
Fails when the category requires capital-intensive distribution or when the founder's circumstances change such that liquidity becomes urgent.

## Counter-evidence
Doist's no-exit posture concedes liquidity events that fund founder reinvestment in adjacent bets. For founders running multiple companies in parallel (the Tiny Capital pattern), a partial exit can fund further calm-company acquisitions.
