# KnowledgeOwl runs on a 25-hour workweek as full-time, started by a customer support hire's request in 2016

## Claim
Marybeth Alexander built KnowledgeOwl on a 25-hour workweek as full-time, after a 2016 hire proposed working 20 hours per week at a healthy hourly rate. The model expanded into company policy when Alexander realized the founders were working more hours than employees and chose to redefine full-time around the team's reality, not the industry's default. Team productivity matches 40-hour comparators while attracting and retaining people who value the time outside work.

## Mechanism
Capping full-time at 25 hours forces ruthless prioritization. Teams spend their hours on what compounds, not on meetings or low-value coordination. Higher hourly rates make the comp competitive with 40-hour roles elsewhere, and the time outside work compounds into life capital (community, side projects, rest) that retains people far better than salary alone. The founder's commitment is the credibility floor: when the founders work less than the team, the policy becomes a perk; when they redefine full-time alongside the team, it becomes culture.

## Conditions
Holds when the work can be organized around outcomes rather than hours, and when the founders accept higher per-hour costs in exchange for retention and quality.
Fails in roles that require physical presence or 24/7 coverage that can't be sliced into 25-hour shifts.

KnowledgeOwl founded 2015, profitable since founding, 10-year anniversary 2025.

## Signals
- Full-time defined as 25 hours per week in employment contracts.
- Hourly rates set above 40-hour comparators to keep total comp competitive.
- Founders work the same hours they ask of the team.

## Counter-evidence
The model raises per-hour costs, which compresses margin in price-sensitive categories. Roles with synchronous-customer-coverage requirements (real-time support, on-call engineering) are harder to fit into 25-hour structures without scaling headcount. The pattern works best in product-led companies where workflow can be asynchronous and outcome-defined.
