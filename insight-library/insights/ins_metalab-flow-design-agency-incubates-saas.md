# MetaLab funded Flow as an in-house product without venture capital, agency revenue covered the build

## Claim
Andrew Wilkinson built MetaLab as a digital-product design agency starting around 2006 and used agency profit to incubate Flow, a task-management product launched March 22, 2011, with no outside investment. The model, services revenue funds product bets, predates the modern studio pattern and shows that calm-company in-house product development can produce shippable software without raising a round.

## Mechanism
A profitable agency throws off cash that's optionality. Founders who allocate that cash to product bets get cheap shots on goal: each product runs on agency overhead rather than burning standalone cash. The agency also generates the design-and-engineering team that builds the product, removing the hiring tax of a fresh startup.

## Conditions
Holds when the agency is profitable enough to absorb product-build cost without compromising client work.
Fails when the in-house product faces a venture-funded competitor in a category with strong network effects (Flow ultimately lost the project-management category to Asana, in part because the calm-company budget couldn't match Asana's distribution spend).

## Evidence
> MetaLab is "a design firm known for work on Qik, Sugar, Texts From Last Night, and the Tumblr theme 'Fluid'."

· TechCrunch, *MetaLab Launches Flow*, March 22, 2011

> Flow "tries to address a common problem: there are plenty of services that offer task management and to-do lists, but people often have their tasks scattered across multiple services."

· TechCrunch, March 22, 2011

Wilkinson later founded Tiny Capital as a holding company for permanently-small profitable internet businesses.

## Signals
- Agency profit allocated explicitly to product bets.
- Product team drawn from agency staff.
- No outside investment on cap table at launch.

## Counter-evidence
Flow's project-management arc (eventually losing to Asana, with Wilkinson publicly writing about $10M+ losses) shows the calm-company-funded product can be outspent in winner-take-most categories. Studio funding works for category-niche products; less well for category-defining bets.
