# Andrew Wilkinson on growth: "I told myself I'd never let the company get bigger than 10 people"

## Claim
Andrew Wilkinson founded MetaLab in 2006 with a self-imposed cap of 10 people, then watched the company grow past it as client demand outpaced his original limit. His 2017 post *Growth Shouldn't Hurt* names the tension: most founders inherit a "more headcount = more value" assumption that breaks the calm-company posture before the founder notices. Wilkinson's later vehicle, Tiny Capital, applies the lesson at portfolio scale: buy and hold permanently small profitable internet businesses, never grow them past their natural size.

## Mechanism
A founder with no explicit headcount target defaults to the industry baseline, which is "hire to keep up with sales." That baseline is invisible until growth produces operating problems (coordination tax, founder distance from customers, culture drift). Naming the cap explicitly forces a different default: solve growth through automation, pricing, and segmentation rather than headcount. Wilkinson's anti-goal framing ("designed my perfect day by fixating on what I hate") generalizes the discipline beyond company size.

## Conditions
Holds when the founder is willing to accept slower revenue growth as the price of cultural integrity.
Fails when category dynamics require capital-intensive distribution that bootstrap pacing can't fund.

## Evidence
> "In the first few years of starting my business, I told myself I'd never let the company get bigger than 10 people."

· Andrew Wilkinson, *Growth Shouldn't Hurt*, awilkinson.medium.com, November 7, 2017

Companion essays from the same archive:

- *The Berkshire Hathaway of The Internet* (March 19, 2017): the Tiny Capital thesis applying Buffett's holding-company model to small profitable internet businesses
- *Skateboard, Bike, Car* (October 23, 2016): incremental product development at MetaLab

Tiny Capital portfolio includes Dribbble, MetaLab, Letterboxd-adjacent properties, and other permanently-small internet businesses.

## Signals
- Founder publishes an explicit headcount cap before crossing it.
- Anti-goals named alongside goals in operating documents.
- Acquisitions evaluated for permanence, not flip potential.

## Counter-evidence
A self-imposed headcount cap can leave revenue on the table when customer demand exceeds what 10-person teams can serve. MetaLab itself eventually grew past Wilkinson's original cap, suggesting the discipline holds best as a ceiling-with-judgment, not as an absolute. Tiny Capital's portfolio approach also requires the holding-company structure and capital availability that single-founder operators don't have.
