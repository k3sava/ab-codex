# 37signals has run Basecamp profitably for 23 years on no outside investment

## Claim
Jason Fried and David Heinemeier Hansson have run 37signals profitably for 23 years, transitioning out of web-design services within a year of Basecamp's release because the product alone outearned the agency. The company has never raised venture capital, never grown headcount-first, and rejects "growth at all costs" as a category error.

## Mechanism
When the product carries the weight, the team doesn't need to be large. Profitability removes the dependency on outside investors that pushes most founders into headcount-first growth. The discipline compounds: every year without a forced growth target is a year of accumulated optionality and culture.

## Conditions
Holds when the product solves a real customer problem and pricing captures durable value.
Fails in winner-take-most categories where competitors with venture capital can subsidize price below sustainable cost.

## Evidence
> "We built Basecamp out of desperate necessity. We needed it bad. Without it, we were embarrassing ourselves."

· Jason Fried, basecamp.com/about

> "About a year or so after we first released it, it was generating more revenue for us than our web design business. So we stopped doing web design and have been focused on building Basecamp ever since."

· Jason Fried, basecamp.com/about

## Signals
- Founder still operating the company two decades in.
- No outside capital on the cap table.
- Profit, not revenue, is the public metric.

## Counter-evidence
The 37signals model concedes winner-take-most categories where venture-subsidized competitors can outspend on distribution. For founders aiming at network-effect or marketplace bets, the calm-company posture undertrades growth optionality.
