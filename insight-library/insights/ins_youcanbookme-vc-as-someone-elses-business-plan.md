# Bridget Harris on outside money: "you have to start to deliver someone else's business plan"

## Claim
Bridget Harris and Keith Harris bootstrapped YouCanBook.Me from 2011 to roughly $5M ARR (acquired by Capacity January 2025) on a refusal to take VC. Bridget's framing makes the no-VC stance specific: outside money obligates the founder to execute someone else's business plan, not their own. The company invested in automation and systems instead of headcount, and the founders went without salaries during the lean years to keep faith with paying customers.

## Mechanism
A founder who takes VC inherits the investor's growth target, not just the investor's cash. The mechanism that follows is structural: investor incentives push for headcount-first scaling and exit-priced returns, which constrains the founder's ability to choose calm-company practices later. Refusing the capital preserves the option set. Building automation instead of teams compounds: each customer added doesn't require a corresponding hire, so the unit economics improve as the product matures.

## Conditions
Holds when the founder has the financial runway (or partner support) to forgo salary during the build period.
Fails when the category requires capital-intensive distribution that bootstrap cashflow can't fund.

· Bridget Harris, youcanbook.me/blog

· Bridget Harris, youcanbook.me/blog (on the early lean years)

Pre-acquisition scale: ~$5M ARR, fully remote, profitable.

## Signals
- Founder operates the company on their own business plan, not a deck pitched to investors.
- Headcount grows slower than customer count; automation absorbs the delta.
- Founders demonstrate skin-in-the-game by forgoing salary during the build phase.

## Counter-evidence
Bridget's stance is reproducible only when the founder has personal runway (employed partner, savings, parallel income) to skip salary during the bootstrap years. For founders without that financial backstop, refusing capital is structurally harder.
