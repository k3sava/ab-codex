# Status quo / no-decision is the real competitor

## Convergence
Across positioning, narrative, homepage, and pitch design, four operators argue that the dominant competitor is not a named alternative — it is the buyer's no-decision. The pitch must first establish that the problem is real and worth solving; only then does feature differentiation pay off.

## Operators
- April Dunford — `ins_no-decision-is-the-real-competitor` and `ins_setup-follow-through-pitch`. 40-60% of B2B buyers say "no decision"; the pitch must run a Setup before the Follow-Through.
- Andy Raskin — `ins_old-game-to-new-game-narrative` and `ins_obstacles-as-gates-not-problems`. Sell the world-shift, not the product comparison; frame features as obstacles to a *new* game, not problems on the current one.
- Anthony Pierri — `ins_homepage-five-second-trinity`. The B2B homepage must communicate use case, alternative, and result in five seconds — without naming the alternative, the buyer does not know what game you're playing.
- Krithika Shankarraman — `ins_use-case-epiphany-as-marketing-job`. Once awareness is solved, marketing's real job is the use-case epiphany — i.e., make the buyer realise the problem is real and worth doing now.

## Variation
- Dunford works the *pitch sequence*.
- Raskin works the *narrative shift*.
- Pierri works the *homepage hierarchy*.
- Shankarraman works the *category-aware moment*.
- Convergence: when the buyer doesn't act, the failure is upstream of feature comparison — the buyer doesn't believe the new game exists.

## Implication
Audit every external surface (homepage, pitch deck, sales call opener) for whether it sells the world-shift before the product. If your message starts with "our features" or "we are better than X," you are pitching to the 40% who already chose the category — not the 60% who haven't decided to play.
