# Claire Hughes Johnson — Scaling People

## Summary
CHJ scaled Stripe from 160 to 7,000+ as COO. Her book Scaling People is a precedent for the operating-system layer of how a young company actually wires goals, cadence, decision rights, and culture into something that doesn't fall over at scale.

## 11 load-bearing insights

1. **Force for positive momentum.** "If you're not sure who the decision maker is, one, it's probably you... Don't get stuck. Too many people get stuck and it makes your work terrible. What do we all care about? Progress, impact, momentum." A mandate for any PM operating without authority.

2. **The house metaphor: foundation, supporting beams, mechanicals.** Foundation = founding documents (mission, long-term goals, operating principles). Supporting beams = goal/QBR/planning structures. Mechanicals = operating cadence (the rhythm of meetings, reviews, demos, customer events). "If you don't start putting those things in early, people will just invent those things, and then you'll have a house that got added on to 17 times."

3. **Levels and ladders earlier than feels comfortable.** Stripe put them in around 200 people; one peer company waited until 800 and called it a bloodbath. "Putting in levels and ladders felt like ripping the bandaid off, honestly, but I was glad we did it." Clearer ownership/leveling early prevents title inflation drama later.

4. **Title minimalism for optionality + culture.** Stripe's "everyone's a product manager / growth lead" pattern is deliberate. "The minute you start titling a lot, you're signaling hierarchy and authority." Two reasons: (a) optionality as the org grows, (b) culture signal that expertise sits with whoever has knowledge, not whoever has the title.

5. **Build self-awareness to build mutual awareness.** First personal operating principle. "The more that you can seek feedback, seek to understand your motivators, your strengths, your blind spots, your tendencies, and take that on board and expose it to others, you're going to be a much more effective company builder."

6. **Say the thing you think you cannot say.** Second personal operating principle. Tactical pattern: (a) ask a question, not a statement; (b) own the observation as your perception, not a judgment. "Lenny, you know what? I wonder if you missed an opportunity in that interview."

7. **Distinguish leader from manager.** Third operating principle. Reid Hoffman example: "I wouldn't hire you to manage McDonald's." Reid was secure enough to hear the feedback because his real operating principle was an environment of open feedback. Leadership work (positioning, voice, taste) is distinct from management work; staff the management layer (project ops, deliverable shepherding) explicitly.

8. **Come back to the operating system.** Fourth operating principle. "Picture yourself spinning out of control. Come back to: this is the order I do things, this is how I get decisions made." Stability through ritual and common practices, not through control.

9. **Hypothesis-based coaching, not data-perfect coaching.** "Take some data, form a hypothesis, and then explore it with the person... a scientific hypothesis is a well-informed piece of intuition." Counter to the "wait until I have a million data points" management orthodoxy. A few data points + a hypothesis beats waiting for statistical significance.

10. **Cadence diagnostics: too fast = no progress between reviews, too slow = stale content.** Stripe sometimes shifted QBRs to every-six-week reviews when product areas demanded it. "If the content you're reviewing seems stale, it's probably too slow." Also: don't let the metrics review become the strategy review by default — that means the strategy review isn't happening often enough.

11. **Don't hire the silver-bullet COO. De-risk with a Head of Business Operations.** "I worry that it's become too much of a panacea... a lower risk strategy could be to bring in a head of business operations or a role that's sort of COO-like and try a couple of functions with someone." A scoped engagement is lower-risk than a panacea hire.

## Other notable threads

- **Communication is a strategic function, not extraneous.** Internal communications gets under-invested early; sales teams already do it well because they have to. "It actually is worth talking to smart sales leaders who've scaled and saying, what do you do that keeps everyone on the same page?"
- **Repeater-in-chief.** Leaders are repeaters. Use different channels — email, video, all-hands, async post — because different teammates absorb differently. "Just like a marketer, use different channels."
- **Make the implicit explicit.** Meeting hygiene rule. Why are we meeting? Who's deciding? What's the criterion? Who's informed?
- **Type 1 vs. Type 2 decisions (Bezos).** High-impact + irreversible vs. reversible. Match the process to the type. Don't drag a Type 2 through committee.
- **Offsites yank people out of routine and imprint memory.** Group experience cements belief or plan.
- **Touchstones (mission / long-term goals / operating principles) as catnip.** "Examples and details and frameworks are catnip." Templates and rubrics are what travel, not the prose.
